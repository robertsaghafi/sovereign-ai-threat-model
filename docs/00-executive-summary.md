\# Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence systems are rapidly evolving from passive
inference services to autonomous or semi-autonomous agents capable of
planning, decision making, and tool execution. These agentic AI systems
interact with enterprise data platforms, external APIs, and distributed
cloud infrastructure.

For organizations operating in regulated environments, the primary
challenge is not only protecting models and data, but preserving
\*\*sovereign control over where data is processed, how models are
deployed, and how automated decisions are governed\*\*.

Traditional threat modeling approaches were designed for software
systems and infrastructure. They do not fully address the risks
introduced by:

\- Agent autonomy and multi-step execution - Prompt injection and
indirect instruction manipulation - Cross-border data processing and
sovereignty constraints - AI model supply chain dependencies - Dynamic
tool invocation within agent workflows

This repository provides a \*\*threat modeling framework tailored for
sovereign AI and agentic AI systems\*\*. The framework enables security
architects, AI platform engineers, and governance leaders to
systematically identify and mitigate risks before systems are deployed.

The approach integrates three core components:

\*\*1. Sovereign AI Threat Taxonomy\*\*

A structured classification of risks specific to sovereign AI systems,
including cross-border inference exposure, jurisdictional compliance
violations, model lifecycle compromise, and agent privilege escalation.

\*\*2. Attack Trees for Agentic Systems\*\*

Threat paths modeling how attackers may exploit AI workflows through
prompt manipulation, toolchain abuse, model tampering, or indirect data
access.

\*\*3. Governance Aligned Control Mapping\*\*

Threat outputs can be mapped to governance frameworks such as:

\- NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) - ISO/IEC 42001 Artificial
Intelligence Management Systems - NIST 800-53 security controls - Zero
Trust architecture principles

This framework allows organizations to move from \*\*theoretical AI
governance\*\* to \*\*actionable security engineering decisions\*\*.

The objective is simple: enable organizations to adopt agentic AI while
maintaining sovereign control over data, models, and operational risk.
